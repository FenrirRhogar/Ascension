1 Introduction
Welcome to the 2026 Graphics Unity Project! This year, we are bringing the excitement of
local multiplayer gaming. Your task is to design and develop a Split-Screen Desktop Game
using the Unity game engine.
In an era dominated by online multiplayer, local split-screen games offer a unique, shared experience, whether it’s the high-fives of a cooperative victory or the friendly rivalry of a competitive
match. You have the creative freedom to choose the direction of your game:
• Cooperative (Co-op): Two players work together to solve puzzles, defeat enemies, or
survive against the odds. (e.g., It Takes Two, Portal 2 ).
• Competitive (PvP): Two players battle against each other for dominance, speed, or
high scores. (e.g., Mario Kart, Rocket League, GoldenEye 007 ).
While split-screen games can be complex, this project focuses on the fundamental mechanics
and components. You are not expected to build a AAA title, but rather to demonstrate your
understanding of Unity’s core systems. You are free to design your own world, characters, and
rules. Use your imagination!
A fully completed game should consist of the following sections and Unity components:
1. Graphics: 3D scene design, 3D objects, textures, materials, lights.
2. Physics: colliders, rigidbodies, forces.
3. Scripting: game logic and mechanics, event handling.
4. Audio: sound effects, music.
5. UI: text display, buttons using Unity’s UI components.

2 Game Overview & Core Components
The concept of the Graphics Course’s project for 2026 is associated with Split-Screen Multiplayer Games. This genre focuses on games where two players share a single screen, typically
divided into independent viewpoints, to enable simultaneous local gameplay.
What is a Split-Screen Multiplayer Desktop game?
A split-screen multiplayer desktop game is a type of game where two players engage in gameplay
on the same computer, with the screen split to provide each player with their own separate
perspective. These games can be competitive, characters play against each other aiming for
victory based on score or objective completion, or collaborative, players work together towards
a common goal, requiring coordination and shared objectives.
The game typically involves:
• Independent Player Control: Each player has full control over their character or
entity.
• Simultaneous Player Input: Both players provide input concurrently.
• Shared Environment: While perspectives are separate, both players exist within the
same game world.
2.1 Case Studies: Famous Split-Screen Games
Analyzing successful split-screen games can provide valuable insight into mechanics and level
design, whether for cooperative or competitive gameplay.
2.1.1 It Takes Two (2021)
• Genre: Action-Adventure Platformer.
• Description: A genre-bending adventure where a couple is turned into dolls.
• Mechanics: Every level introduces new mechanics that require specific cooperation (e.g.,
one player has a hammer head, the other has nails).
• It is the gold standard for modern split-screen, forcing players to communicate and work
together constantly.
2.1.2 Mario Kart 8 Deluxe (2017)
• Genre: Kart Racing.
• Description: Players race against each other on various tracks using items to hinder
opponents.
• Mechanics: Split-screen allows players to see their own view while being aware of their
opponent’s position.
• A classic example of competitive split-screen where screen peeking is part of the strategy.
2.1.3 Portal 2 (2011)
• Genre: Puzzle-Platformer.
• Description: Two robots navigate complex test chambers.

• Mechanics: Players must use portals to solve physics-based puzzles, often requiring
precise timing and trust.
• Perfect example of how to use split-screen for puzzle solving.
2.1.4 Halo: Combat Evolved (2001)
• Genre: First-Person Shooter (FPS).
• Description: Classic shooter that defined console multiplayer.
• Mechanics: Halo’s campaign allowed two players to fight through the story together,

• The game shows the foundation of split-screen: shared screen real estate and the fun of
local social play.
2.2 Playable and Non-Playable Characters
The game will contain the following characters:
• Playable Characters (PCs). The PCs are fictional characters, whose actions are controlled by the players. Since this is a split-screen game, there are two distinct PCs (Player
1 and Player 2). They need to cooperate or compete to pass through several levels to
fulfill the goal of the game. The PCs enter a scene and need to complete specific objectives (e.g., reach an exit, defeat the opponent, score points) to progress or win. In
their way there might be physical obstacles, while the PCs may interact with objects and
non-playable characters (NPCs). Each PC has a healthbar and a basic inventory. They
can get consumables from the scene, which can be added to their inventory.
• Non-Playable Characters (NPCs). Those are game objects with some basic form of
artificial intelligence (AI). NPCs can be of different types and have various interactions.
e.g., they can interact with the PCs, by helping or attacking them. However, all NPCs
must have boundaries based on the type of the aspects of your game.
2.3 Interactable and Non-Interactable Objects
The game will contain the following type of objects:
• Interactable objects. Like any other game, there must be some objects that the player
can interact with. Those can be movable rocks, unlockable doors, opening chests, growing
plants, etc. The task of the player is to interact with those objects.
• Physical obstacles. Those are immovable objects that block the path of the player (e.g.,
trees, rocks, etc.).
• Consumables & Power-ups. These are objects located in the scene that apply a
temporary or permanent effect to the player. They can be:
– Instant: Activated immediately upon contact (e.g., a speed pad on the ground, a
health pack, or a trap that slows movement).
– Inventory Items: Collected and stored for later use (e.g., a potion, a shield, or a
projectile).
Consumables can have positive effects like increased speed, strength, or invulnerability,
or negative effects like inverted controls, slowed movement, or vision obstruction.

2.4 Commands
The players must be able to use specific commands. Core commands include, but are not limited
to, the following:
• Move Command: Each player can move their playable character to a specific destination. This movement is typically done with WASD keys for Player 1 and Arrow Keys for
Player 2, or using gamepads.
• Interact with Object Command: The playable characters can interact with other
objects which are part of a solvable puzzle. Such examples are: grab a rock and move it,
use a key on a door to open it etc.
• Use Consumable Command: The playable characters can use an item from their
inventory or activate a consumable in the environment. This includes consuming a potion,
activating a power-up, or triggering a temporary effect. Note that some consumable might
be activated automatically upon collision (e.g., walking over a speed pad).
• Interact with NPC/Player Command: The playable characters can interact with
NPCs or the other player. NPCs can be either hostile or friendly. A classic example is
the attack command where a player can attack NPCs or the opponent.
2.5 Level Design
The level design is a scene with a defined area, an entrance or spawn points and exit for the
playable character. The scene is filled with physical obstacles, interactable objects, consumables
and NPCs. The playable character(s) will enter from the starting position and aim to complete
the level’s objective. The main objective is to unlock the exit by achieving a set of conditions
like solving a puzzle or defeating the opponent.
2.6 Camera Control
In a split-screen game, the camera system is crucial for providing each player with a clear view
of their character and surroundings. You must implement a dual-camera setup:
• Split-Screen Setup: You need two separate Camera objects in your scene.
– Player 1 Camera: Renders to the left (or top) half of the screen.
– Player 2 Camera: Renders to the right (or bottom) half of the screen.
• Camera Movement: The camera should smoothly follow its assigned player. Depending
on your game design, you may allow players to rotate the camera around their character
(e.g., using designated controls for each player) to get a better view of the environment.
2.7 AI Components
Non-Playable Characters (NPCs) bring life to your game world. They should exhibit intelligent
behavior, such as navigating the environment and reacting to the players.
2.7.1 AI Navigation
NPCs must be able to move around the level intelligently without walking through walls or
falling off the map. To achieve this, you should use Unity’s NavMesh System:
• Bake the NavMesh: Mark your floors and ramps as “Navigation Static” and bake the
NavMesh to define walkable areas.
7
• NavMesh Agent: Add this component to your NPCs so they can calculate paths and
move towards a target destination automatically avoiding obstacles.
2.7.2 AI Behaviors
NPCs should follow a logic structure, often called a Finite State Machine (FSM), to decide
what to do based on the game context.
Common NPC States:
• Idle: The NPC stands still, looks around, or plays an idle animation.
• Patrol / Wander: The NPC moves between predefined points or wanders randomly
within an area.
• Approach / Follow: If a player enters a “detection radius”, the NPC moves towards
them. This could be a monster chasing a player or a pet following its owner.
• Action / Interaction: When close enough, the NPC performs a specific action. This
could be attacking, starting a dialogue, trading items, healing the player or something
entierly different (Up to you!).
Example Scenarios:
• Hostile (Guard): Patrols a hallway. If it spots a player, it switches to Chase. Upon
catching the player, it switches to Attack.
• Friendly (Shopkeeper): Stands in Idle behind a counter. When a player approaches,
it switches to Greet/Trade, opening a shop UI.
• Quest NPC: Stands in a specific location. When a player approaches, it switches to
Dialogue, allowing the player to receive or complete quests.
• Neutral (Pet): Wanders around (Patrol). When the player calls it, it switches to Follow.
2.8 Audio: Music and Sound Effects
Audio is a vital component that breathes life into your game, setting the mood and providing
feedback to the player. You should implement two distinct types of audio:
• Background Music: A looping track that sets the atmosphere of the level (e.g., tense
music for a boss fight, calm music for a puzzle).
• Sound Effects (SFX): Short audio clips triggered by specific events to provide immediate
feedback. Examples include:
– Interaction: Picking up a coin, opening a door, pressing a button.
– Movement: Footsteps, jumping, landing.
– Combat/Action: Swinging a sword, shooting, taking damage.
Unity Implementation Tip: Use Audio Source components to play sounds. For background music, set the Audio Source to “Loop”. For SFX, use PlayOneShot() in your scripts.
(Optional) Consider using 3D Sound settings for in-game objects so players can hear where a
sound is coming from (spatial audio).

2.9 User Interface
You should implement the basic UI functionality required for your game to be played smoothly.
Don’t take anything for granted and explain everything in your game using UIs or by guiding
your players in a smart way. Show at every stage of the game the progression to the players, and

(e.g., show healthbar, available consumables, etc). Since this is a split-screen game, ensure UI
elements are positioned correctly for each player’s view.

It is a very broad guideline that dictates the core design principles of the development process
and helps guide us when coming up with new ideas or when searching for assets.

by the players?), Setting (In what location and time period does the game take place? What is
the main visual theme or technological background?), Goal (what do the players seek to achieve

6 Basic Implementation Instructions
This section provides a guide to the fundamental Unity concepts you will need to implement your
project. For detailed information, always refer to the Unity Manual and Scripting API.
6.1 3D Models and Environment
Creating a visually appealing world is the first step in game development. You have two main
options for acquiring 3D assets: creating them yourself or importing them.
If you are comfortable with 3D modeling software like Blender, you can create custom models and import them into Unity. For those who prefer to stay within the Unity Editor, the
ProBuilder package is an excellent tool for gray-boxing and creating level geometry directly in
the scene. Alternatively, you can download high-quality assets from the Unity Asset Store or
websites like Sketchfab. Remember to credit any third-party assets you use.
To make your objects look realistic, you must apply Materials. A Material defines how a
surface renders, including its color, texture, and smoothness. You can create a new Material in
the Project window (Create ¿ Material) and drag it onto your object.
Unity Documentation: Materials
6.2 Physics System
Unity’s built-in physics engine handles collisions and movement simulation. Understanding the
relationship between Colliders and Rigidbodies is crucial.
6.2.1 Colliders
A Collider component defines the physical shape of an object for collision purposes. It does
not need to match the visual mesh exactly; often, a simple primitive (Box, Sphere, Capsule) is
more efficient and sufficient for gameplay. Without a Collider, objects will pass through each
other like ghosts.
Unity Documentation: Colliders
6.2.2 Rigidbodies
The Rigidbody component enables an object to be affected by physical forces, such as gravity
and collisions. If you want an object to fall, be pushed, or interact physically with the player,
it must have a Rigidbody. You can adjust parameters like Mass (how heavy it is) and Drag
(air resistance) to tune the feel of the object.
Unity Documentation: Rigidbody
6.3 Scripting and Game Logic
Scripts are the brain of your game. Written in C#, they control object behavior, handle input,
and manage game rules. In Unity, scripts typically derive from the MonoBehaviour class, which
allows them to attach to GameObjects and execute code during specific game events.
6.3.1 Key Lifecycle Methods
• Start(): Called once when the script instance is enabled, before the first frame update.
Use this for initialization, such as setting up variables or finding references to other objects.
• Update(): Called once per frame. This is where you handle user input (e.g., Input.GetAxis)
and non-physics logic (e.g., timers, simple movement).
14
• FixedUpdate(): Called at fixed time intervals. All physics-related calculations (e.g.,
adding force to a Rigidbody) should happen here to ensure consistent simulation.
6.3.2 Component Communication
Scripts often need to interact with other components on the same object or different objects. The
GetComponent<Type>() method is the primary way to access other components. For example,
to make a character jump, your script would get a reference to the Rigidbody component and
apply an upward force.
Unity Documentation: Scripting Overview
6.4 User Interface (UI)
The UI system allows you to display information like health bars, scores, and menus. Unity
uses a Canvas system to render 2D elements over the 3D game world. Key components include
Text (for displaying strings), Images (for icons and bars), and Buttons (for interaction). You
can anchor these elements to different parts of the screen to ensure they look correct on different
resolutions.
Unity Documentation: User Interface
6.5 Audio
Sound is essential for immersion. The audio system relies on two main components:
• Audio Listener: Acts as the “ears” of the world. By default, this is attached to the
Main Camera.
• Audio Source: Emits sound. You attach this to objects that make noise (e.g., the player,
an explosion, a radio).
You can play sounds automatically on start (for background music) or trigger them via scripts
using methods like Play() or PlayOneShot().
Unity Documentation: Audio

7 Useful tips
• It is highly recommended to use the Unity Documentation as it provides both explanations
on all of Unity’s tools as well as examples on how to use them. When you open the
Documentation remember to select your Unity Version on the top left.
• It is highly recommended to give your executable version of your game to be tested by
others without your guidance. Try to evaluate your game utilizing the thinking-aloud
method. In a thinking-aloud test, you ask test participants to use the system while
continuously thinking out loud — that is, simply verbalizing their thoughts as they move
through the user interface.
– Don’t trust your instincts on the quality of your UIs implementation. User experience
is crucial during the testing of your game. Put effort into the creation of your UI/UX
and make it as intuitive as possible.
• During the exam, you will be graded based on the playable version of your game. Anything
you implement that cannot be shown while playing the game will not be graded. As such
you should take into consideration:
– Someone playing the game should be able to see everything you want to show in a
maximum of 10 minutes. You will be able to give us advice while playing your game
in order to achieve this.
• Excluding extreme circumstances, we will not view your project through the Unity Editor
so make sure your build is fully playable! Some common issues that may appear include
the following:
– UI elements may not scale correctly with your screen resolution and be out of the
playable field. Make sure your game plays correctly at least for the recommended
resolution of Full HD (1920x1080) or smaller. If you have a specific resolution you
tested your game at and it works correctly other than Full HD specify it on your
report.
– Sometimes things that work in the Editor do not work the same way after you build
the game. Always build and test your game during development! The most common
issues involve the initialization of objects while switching between scenes e.g. when
transitioning from the main menu to the game or when restarting the game and
reloading the same scene.
• It is recommended you watch online tutorials and even exchange ideas with each other
but plagiarism is strictly forbidden. Additionally, if you get some piece of code from
the internet and you cannot explain it, you will not receive any points for the respective
component.

8 Project Guidelines
• Your code must be organized in different scripts, depending on the functionality. Each
script must have a distinct role.
• Use script names that make sense and attach them to related game objects. For example,
create a ”UIManager” script to handle the game UI, a ”GameManager” script to hold
variables and objects that keep track of the game’s state.
• Your code must be written in C# programming language ONLY.
• Organize your game objects in a hierarchy that makes sense and is easy to understand.
For example, if you have game objects such as ”Cube(1)”, ”Cube(2)”, ”Cube(3)”, ... ,
make them children of a single ”CUBES” game object.
• Organize your asset files (textures, 3d models, materials, scripts, sounds, ...) in separate
folders. Obviously, the name of the folder should be representative of the content.
• You may use the Internet (YouTube, blogs, ...) for tutorials and code examples, but
copy-pasting entire code or functionality is obvious and will NOT be accepted.
• You cannot use “Complete Game Kits” or plugins that do everything for you (e.g., a “FPS
All-in-One Kit” that handles movement, shooting, and AI automatically).
Gamepad Support Please be advised that while you may choose to implement gamepad
support for split-screen controls, it is mandatory to also provide keyboard and mouse shortcuts
to ensure your game can be tested fairly by the instructors. Additionally, please note that
implementing these alternative controls for the camera setup will not grant any extra points for
the camera component or bonus marks beyond the basic requirements.
LLM Usage Policy Use of LLMs (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot) is permitted
